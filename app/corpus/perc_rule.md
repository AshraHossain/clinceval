# Pulmonary Embolism Rule-out Criteria (PERC Rule)

## Purpose
The PERC rule is used to rule out pulmonary embolism (PE) in patients who are considered low risk based on clinical judgment. If a patient meets all 8 criteria, no further diagnostic workup for PE (such as D-dimer or CTPA) is indicated, avoiding unnecessary testing.

## Inputs Required
- Age (< 50 years)
- Heart rate (< 100 beats per minute)
- Oxygen saturation (SpO2 >= 95% on room air)
- Prior history of DVT or PE
- Recent trauma or surgery within the last 4 weeks (requiring hospitalization or anesthesia)
- Hemoptysis (coughing up blood)
- Exogenous estrogen use (oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy, or transdermal estrogen)
- Unilateral leg swelling (visual asymmetry of the calves)

## Scoring Logic
The rule is applied to patients who are already stratified as low risk for PE (e.g., clinician gestates < 15% probability of PE).
A patient must meet ALL 8 criteria to be "PERC negative":
1. Age < 50 years
2. Heart rate < 100 bpm
3. SpO2 >= 95% on room air
4. No prior DVT or PE
5. No recent trauma or surgery (within 4 weeks)
6. No hemoptysis
7. No exogenous estrogen use
8. No unilateral leg swelling

If any criterion is NOT met (i.e. if the answer is "yes" to a risk factor or the thresholds are breached), the patient is "PERC positive."

## Interpretation Bands
- **PERC Negative** (meets all 8 criteria): Post-test probability of PE is extremely low (< 1%). No further diagnostic evaluation for PE is required.
- **PERC Positive** (fails 1 or more criteria): The rule cannot be used to exclude PE. Further testing (typically D-dimer or imaging) is needed.
